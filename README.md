# Quanser Aero PID Control Project

**AE 443 – Experimental Dynamics & Controls**
**Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University**

This repository documents a controls lab in which a PID controller was designed, implemented, and tuned for the pitch axis of a Quanser Aero — a two-thruster, 1-degree-of-freedom (1-DOF) aerodynamic testbed — using MATLAB/Simulink and QUARC real-time control software.

---

## 1. System Overview

The Quanser Aero consists of two DC-motor-driven propeller thrusters mounted on a pivoting body. In the 1-DOF configuration used for this project, the body rotates about the **pitch axis**, and the two thrusters act in opposition to control the pitch angle.

Key modeling relationships:

- **Motor electrical dynamics** — governed by Kirchhoff's Voltage Law, relating applied voltage, back-EMF, and motor current.
- **Motor mechanical dynamics** — torque generated by motor current, opposed by drag/air-resistance torque proportional to shaft speed.
- **Body torque balance** — thrust forces from each propeller act at a fixed displacement from the pivot, opposed by gravity acting on the offset center of mass.
- **Control variable** — the two motor voltages are combined into a single differential control input, `Vm`, since the thrusters act in opposition on the same axis.

These relationships combine into a governing torque equation that forms the basis for treating the Aero as a controllable dynamic plant.

## 2. Part 1 — Qualitative PID Control

### 2.1 Open-Loop Response
The system was first run open-loop: a step input (10V → 15V) was applied directly to the motor voltage, and the resulting pitch angle response was recorded. This baseline response was used to measure peak time, settling time, overshoot, natural frequency, and damping ratio — establishing that the uncontrolled system is lightly damped and oscillates significantly before settling.

### 2.2 Proportional (P) Control
A proportional gain block (`Kp`) was inserted into the unity-feedback loop between the error signal and the motor voltage command. `Kp` was varied between 5 and 10 to observe its effect:
- Increasing `Kp` reduces rise time (faster response).
- However, it also increases overshoot and sustains oscillations, since a P-only controller provides no damping mechanism and cannot eliminate steady-state error.

### 2.3 Derivative (D) Control
A derivative gain block (`Kd`), with a filtered transfer function to reduce encoder noise sensitivity, was added in parallel with the proportional term. With `Kp` fixed at 10, `Kd` was varied between 4 and 8 using a 0.3 rad square wave input:
- Increasing `Kd` reduces overshoot and stabilizes oscillations by acting as added damping.
- A minimum `Kd` was identified as the smallest value that kept peak pitch within a ±0.35 rad limit; below this threshold, the system re-exhibited sustained, lightly-damped oscillation.

### 2.4 Integral (I) Control
With `Kp` and `Kd` held at their tuned values, an integral gain block (`Ki`) was added in parallel to eliminate residual steady-state error (present even with a well-tuned PD controller). `Ki` was varied between 3 and 6:
- Increasing `Ki` drives the steady-state response closer to the commanded value.
- Excessive `Ki` reintroduces overshoot and slows settling.
- A specific `Ki` value was identified that brought the steady-state response within a required ±3% band of the command.

### 2.5 Response Tuning
All three gains (`Kp`, `Ki`, `Kd`) were jointly fine-tuned to simultaneously satisfy:
- Peak time < 1.1 s
- Peak pitch within ±0.35 rad
- Negligible steady-state error

The final tuned response was plotted against the command signal and overshoot limits, and the resulting gains were recorded as the empirically tuned PID controller.

## 3. Part 2 — PID Control to Specification

### 3.1 Second-Order System Approximation
Since a full physical model of the Aero is high-order (3rd–4th order) and impractical for classical compensator design, the plant was instead approximated as a second-order system:

1. The PID controller was reset to unity feedback (`Kp = 1`, `Ki = Kd = 0`).
2. A unit step response was recorded and analyzed to extract peak time, peak value, and final (steady-state) value.
3. These values were used to back-calculate the natural frequency (`ωn`) and damping ratio (`ζ`) of an equivalent second-order model.
4. Because the steady-state value did not equal 1 (unity DC gain), a modified second-order transfer function was used to account for the actual final value.
5. The resulting transfer function was implemented in Simulink and compared against the real plant response to validate the approximation.

### 3.2 Analytical PID Gain Calculation
Using the second-order plant model, PID gains were calculated analytically via pole-placement:

- The closed-loop characteristic equation of the compensated system (3rd order) was matched to a prototypical third-order form with a real pole (`p0`) and a complex conjugate pair defined by `ωn` and `ζ`.
- Design targets: **critical damping (ζ = 1)** with a **5% settling time of 2 seconds**, giving `ωn ≈ 2 rad/s`, and a zero position `p0 = 1`.
- Closed-form expressions for `Kp`, `Ki`, and `Kd` in terms of the plant coefficients (`a`, `b`, `c`) and design parameters (`ωn`, `ζ`, `p0`) were used to compute the gains.
- These analytically-derived gains were then applied to the real system (0.3 rad square wave input) and evaluated against the same damping and settling-time specifications.

## 4. Key Takeaways

- **Proportional-only control** cannot simultaneously achieve fast response and low overshoot — it lacks damping and steady-state correction.
- **Derivative control** is essential for damping oscillations introduced by proportional gain.
- **Integral control** removes steady-state error but must be balanced carefully, as excess integral action reintroduces overshoot.
- **Empirical (iterative) tuning** and **analytical (pole-placement) design** can produce noticeably different gain sets depending on the design objective — iterative tuning driven by a fast peak-time requirement favors much higher proportional gain, while analytical design targeting critical damping favors a smoother, slower response.
- A **second-order approximation**, while not capturing all physical dynamics (e.g., motor inductance, propeller aerodynamics), is sufficient as a design basis for classical PID synthesis.
- Extending the analysis into the **frequency domain** (Bode plots for stability margins/bandwidth) and **state-space** (controllability, observability, LQR) provides complementary perspectives on controller performance and actuator feasibility beyond what time-domain tuning alone reveals.
